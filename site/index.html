Excerpt from Mark Twain's essay "The Cat," where he discusses the behavior and character of cats

When a man loves cats, I am his comrade without further introduction. I have, met a good many people in my time who were supposed to be hard-hearted, but I never saw one of them that was not particularly fond of cats. The aloofness and otherworldly nature of cats has made them the object of both affection and curiosity for countless writers, and Mark Twain's writings on the subject are just one example.

The Naming of Cats is a difficult matter, It isn't just one of your holiday games; You may think at first I'm as mad as a hatter When I tell you a cat must have THREE DIFFERENT NAMES.

Ernest Hemingway had a well-documented interest in cats

In the tropical haven of Key West, Florida, Hemingway surrounded himself with the company of polydactyl (six-toed) cats. To him, these cats were not merely pets but beloved companions that brought him comfort and solace amidst his adventurous and often tumultuous life. Hemingway's interest in cats, like his writing, reflected a complex duality: the man of action and the man who found serenity in the company of these independent, enigmatic creatures."
